# Humanize Writing — Before/After Patterns

All examples are drawn from a real CSR ethics essay revision session.

---

## Pattern 1: First-Person Injection

Add "I think", "in my view", "honestly", "to me" at moments of conviction. Don't overdo it — 3-5 instances per essay is enough.

**After hook numbers:**
> BEFORE: "That gap is not a procedural technicality. Under CSR theory, it is an ethical failure."
> AFTER: "That gap is not a procedural technicality. Under CSR theory, it is an ethical failure, and I think the numbers make that case better than any legal argument could."

**After stark facts:**
> BEFORE: "That is not a neutral procedural design. That is the point of the policy."
> AFTER: "In my view, that is not a side effect of the policy. It is the design."

**After surprising data:**
> BEFORE: "E&Y did not just reduce its exposure. It nearly eliminated it."
> AFTER: "Honestly, that is a striking outcome for a company that may have owed its workers millions. E&Y did not just reduce its exposure. It nearly eliminated it."

**In a rebuttal:**
> BEFORE: "None of these arguments holds up."
> AFTER: "I believe the legality argument is the weakest of the three."

**In the conclusion:**
> BEFORE: "A program optimized to discourage enforcement is not a fair process."
> AFTER: "To me, a program that works by discouraging people from coming forward is not a fair process by any definition."

## Pattern 2: Structural Colon Replacement

Colons used mid-sentence as "here comes the elaboration" punctuation. Replace with a period and a new sentence, or restructure.

**Simple period swap:**
> BEFORE: "the policy implications here are real: when claims are resolved privately, organizations face less pressure"
> AFTER: "the policy implications here are real. When claims are resolved privately, organizations face less pressure"

**Restructure to add voice:**
> BEFORE: "Friedman's framework provides a coherent defense: if the executive's duty is to maximize profits within the law..."
> AFTER: "Friedman's framework provides a coherent defense. His logic is that if the executive's duty is to maximize profits within the law..."

**In definitions:**
> BEFORE: "The first is distributive fairness: how the policy allocates gains and burdens"
> AFTER: "The first is distributive fairness, which is really just asking who wins and who loses under the policy."

---

## Pattern 3: "On X:" Checklist to Prose

Mechanical rebuttal structure that reads like a formatted list even when written out. Break each item into a full sentence that leads with a human observation.

> BEFORE:
> "On the Friedman argument: even Friedman acknowledged..."
> "On arbitrator expertise: skilled arbitrators are perfectly capable..."
> "On efficiency: a consequence-based analysis asks..."

> AFTER:
> "Starting with Friedman's argument, even he acknowledged..."
> "The arbitrator expertise point is worth taking seriously, but skilled arbitrators can hear class actions when they are allowed to."
> "The efficiency argument is the most revealing of the three. A consequence-based analysis asks..."

The key move: give each rebuttal a one-line editorial judgment before the evidence ("worth taking seriously, but", "most revealing of the three").

## Pattern 4: Textbook Phrasing to Plain English

Replace formal vocabulary that sounds like it was copied from a textbook.

| Textbook phrase | Plain English |
|---|---|
| "vindicate their rights" | "get what they were owed" / "enforce their rights" |
| "externalize costs" | "pass the costs onto workers" |
| "allocates gains and burdens" | "who wins and who loses" |
| "a realistic path to vindication" | "workers can actually use their rights" |
| "governance choices that suppress grievances" | "policies that keep complaints quiet" |
| "sustains the conditions for accountability" | "keeps firms accountable" |
| "under-enforcement of statutory rights" | "workers not getting what the law says they're owed" |
| "the moral minimum" | keep — this one is short and clear |
| "It asks whether..." (repeated) | "The question is whether..." or rewrite to vary |
| "It concerns how..." | just say what it concerns in plain terms |

**Full paragraph example:**
> BEFORE: "The second is procedural fairness. It asks whether workers have a realistic, not merely theoretical, path to enforce their rights."
> AFTER: "The second is procedural fairness, meaning whether workers can actually use their rights, not just technically have them."

## Pattern 5: Sentence Rhythm

AI prose tends toward uniform sentence length. Fix by alternating long and short.

**Short sentences for verdicts:**
> "E&Y did not just reduce its exposure. It nearly eliminated it. In my view, that is not a side effect of the policy. It is the design."

Short sentences work best AFTER a longer setup sentence. Never stack more than two in a row or it becomes choppy.

**Leading with a short hook:**
> "A right you cannot afford to exercise is not much of a right."
> "It is a liability shield."
> "That is not a neutral procedural choice. It extracts a concession."

---

## Pattern 6: Transitions That Don't Announce Themselves

AI writing uses transitions that explicitly signal the structure. Real writing just moves.

| AI transition | Human transition |
|---|---|
| "The procedural fairness problem follows directly." | "The procedural fairness problem is just as serious." |
| "Deterrence is the systemic dimension of this analysis." | "The deterrence issue is broader still." |
| "Turning now to the counterarguments..." | Just start the counterargument paragraph |
| "Having established X, we can now see Y..." | Cut entirely — start the new point |

Rule: if the transition could be a section header, it's too structured. Either cut it or rewrite it as an opinion ("The deterrence issue is broader still" sounds like a person; "Deterrence is the systemic dimension" sounds like an outline).

---

## Pattern 7: Letting Numbers Do Dramatic Work

Don't bury numbers inside analytical sentences. Lead with the number, then explain it.

> BEFORE: "The expected liability under a class action was substantially higher than what E&Y ultimately paid, which demonstrates the policy's financial effectiveness."

> AFTER: "If the class action had proceeded and won, expected liability was $25 million. E&Y paid $650,000. E&Y did not just reduce its exposure. It nearly eliminated it."

The number itself makes the argument. The prose just needs to say what the number means — not analyze it to death.

---

## Pattern 8: Conviction Without Perfect Hedging

AI writing hedges everything equally. Real writers get a little heated when the facts are stark.

> BEFORE: "None of these arguments is sufficient to overcome the ethical concerns raised by the policy."
> AFTER: "None of these arguments actually holds up on these facts."

> BEFORE: "The efficiency gains are ethically questionable given the design of the policy."
> AFTER: "The efficiency gains here are ethically tainted because the streamlined process was designed to suppress claims, not resolve them fairly."

The difference: "ethically questionable" is careful. "Ethically tainted" takes a position. When the facts warrant it, take the position.
